Overview Attack & Impact Response Frameworks Leadership Recommendations
Controlled response

Incident response must be visible, coordinated, and evidence-led

The response model should protect people, preserve evidence, restore services safely, and maintain management accountability. It must also recognise that ransomware response is not only technical; communication, compliance, finance, welfare, and supplier coordination matter throughout the lifecycle.

Preparation

Build response before the crisis

Maintain an offline playbook, escalation contacts, role-specific checklists, backup evidence, supplier routes, insurer information, and tabletop exercises. Preparation is what makes response practical under pressure.

Detection

Define scope quickly

Identify suspicious access, outages, disabled protections, unusual data movement, and service impact quickly so management understands severity, affected systems, and potential personal data exposure.

Containment

Limit spread without losing evidence

Isolate affected systems, disable compromised accounts, restrict risky remote access, and preserve logs and forensic evidence so the threat is slowed without destroying the record of what happened.

Eradication

Remove unsafe persistence

Remove malware, persistence mechanisms, unsafe access paths, and compromised credentials. Systems that cannot be trusted should be rebuilt from clean baselines rather than assumed safe.

Recovery

Restore in business order

Restore services from clean and tested backups in a business-led order, beginning with critical functions such as identity, communications, website, inventory, and essential store operations.

Lessons learned

Translate evidence into resilience

Brief the board on root cause, response quality, legal exposure, supplier issues, cost, staff impact, and required investment so the organisation becomes stronger after stabilisation.
