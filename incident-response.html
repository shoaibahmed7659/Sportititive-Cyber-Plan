Controlled ransomware response

Incident response must be visible, coordinated and evidence-led

The response model should protect people, preserve evidence, restore services safely and maintain management accountability. It should also recognise that ransomware response is not only technical; communication, compliance, finance, welfare and supplier coordination matter throughout the incident lifecycle.

Preparation

Maintain an offline playbook, escalation contacts, role-specific checklists, backup evidence, supplier routes, insurer information and tabletop exercises. Preparation is what makes response practical under pressure.

Detection

Identify suspicious access, outages, disabled protections, unusual data movement and service impact quickly. Management must know what is affected, how severe the issue is and whether personal data may be involved.

Containment

Isolate affected systems, disable compromised accounts, restrict risky remote access and preserve logs and forensic evidence. This phase should reduce spread without destroying the record of what happened.

Eradication

Remove malware, persistence mechanisms, unsafe access paths and compromised credentials. Systems that cannot be trusted should be rebuilt from clean baselines rather than assumed to be safe.

Recovery

Restore services from clean and tested backups in a business-led order, beginning with critical functions such as identity, communications, website, inventory and essential store operations. Recovery should happen alongside enhanced monitoring.

Lessons learned

Brief the board on root cause, response quality, legal exposure, supplier issues, cost, staff impact and required investment. The playbook, training and governance metrics should then be updated so the organisation becomes more resilient.
