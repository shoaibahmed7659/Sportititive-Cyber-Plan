Response mind-map

Six branches every leader can follow.

The plan is arranged around one central objective: restore safe business operations while protecting customers, employees, evidence and board accountability.

Controlled ransomware response

Protect people, preserve evidence, restore services and document decisions.

Operational detail

Phase-by-phase action plan.

The interactive view below gives each phase enough detail for decision ownership without overwhelming the viewer.

Preparation

Build the response before the crisis: owners, contact lists, insurance details, Police and Action Fraud routes, ICO escalation, backup evidence and staff rota planning.

  • Maintain offline playbook copies, supplier contacts and board escalation paths.
  • Test backup restoration quarterly and keep backup credentials separate from domain admin.
  • Run board tabletop exercises and staff phishing simulations.

Detection and analysis

Confirm what is happening, how severe it is, what systems and data are affected, and whether the attacker still has access.

  • Review authentication, firewall, VPN, cloud and endpoint logs.
  • Preserve evidence before wiping machines.
  • Assess whether personal data exposure triggers ICO and individual notification duties.

Containment and eradication

Limit spread, remove access and stop the attacker from using the environment while preserving evidence where possible.

  • Isolate infected hosts, disable compromised accounts and block risky remote access.
  • Segment affected networks and restrict SMB or lateral movement paths where needed.
  • Remove malware, rotate credentials and rebuild from known-clean images.

Recovery

Restore business services in a controlled order from clean, tested backups and monitor heavily during return to service.

  • Prioritise identity services, communications, IMS, web services and critical store operations.
  • Scan backups before restoration and validate integrity after restore.
  • Keep enhanced monitoring active to detect re-entry attempts.

Communication

Communicate honestly without speculation. A named lead should coordinate staff, customers, regulators, insurers, suppliers and media updates.

  • Tell staff what is known, what they should do, and where to report concerns.
  • Notify affected individuals where risk to rights and freedoms requires it.
  • Record key decisions, including why ransom contact or payment was rejected.

Lessons learned

Within two weeks of stabilisation, brief the board on root cause, response quality, legal exposure, cost, wellbeing impact and control investments.

  • Update DPIAs, supplier risk records, policies and the playbook.
  • Track corrective actions to closure through a cyber dashboard.
  • Run a follow-up exercise after improvements are implemented.
Decision ownership

Who needs to act?

A ransomware response fails when every decision is treated as technical. The model below shows which leadership role should own each part of the response.

CIO

Response governance

Own the playbook, chair the response rhythm, brief the board and maintain the decision log.

CTO

Technical recovery

Coordinate containment, clean rebuilds, restoration sequence, monitoring and forensic evidence.

Compliance

Regulatory evidence

Guide DPIA updates, breach records, ICO notification and customer communication triggers.

HR

People impact

Support staff communications, rota pressure, wellbeing, awareness and social engineering risk.

Board

Risk appetite

Approve major decisions, challenge evidence gaps and fund priority recovery improvements.

Suppliers

Third-party control

Provide access records, recovery support, assurance evidence and contractually required cooperation.
