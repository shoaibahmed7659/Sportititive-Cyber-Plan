CIO implementation roadmap

Turn the response into a funded resilience programme.

The CIO role is to connect cyber controls to business risk, governance, supplier assurance, recovery confidence and board-level investment decisions.

View phase map Board reporting model
Phase-wise delivery

Implementation phases by CIO priority.

The roadmap is intentionally phased so urgent risk reduction happens first, then the organisation builds stronger controls, and finally it modernises the legacy constraints that allowed the incident to spread.

0-3 months

Stabilise

Reduce immediate recurrence risk and give the board confidence that recovery basics are controlled.

  • Enforce MFA for cloud, admin, VPN and MSP access.
  • Confirm backup integrity and protect backup credentials.
  • Remove unused privileged access and review supplier accounts.
  • Start mandatory awareness training and phishing reporting.
3-12 months

Build defence-in-depth

Move from reactive fixes to a structured control model aligned to Zero Trust and ransomware readiness.

  • Deploy segmentation for stores, HQ, cloud, POS and backup environments.
  • Introduce EDR where supported and centralise security logging.
  • Run supplier access reviews and contract assurance checks.
  • Run formal ransomware tabletop exercises with board involvement.
12-24 months

Transform

Address legacy architecture and turn cyber resilience into an ongoing management discipline.

  • Replace or re-platform legacy POS constraints.
  • Mature Zero Trust across identity, devices, network zones and suppliers.
  • Introduce cyber dashboards for risk, incidents, supplier posture and recovery testing.
  • Independently assure critical supplier controls.
CIO ownership model

What the CIO must own and influence.

The CIO does not personally perform every technical task. The role is to create the governance, funding and accountability model that makes the controls sustainable.

Core role

Business-aligned cyber strategy

Translate ransomware risk into a board-level programme that protects revenue, customers, staff, legal standing and operational continuity.

Governance

Decision records

Maintain evidence for ransom decisions, ICO notification, DPIAs, supplier exceptions and investment trade-offs.

Budget

Risk-based investment

Prioritise MFA, backup protection, monitoring, segmentation and legacy POS renewal by risk reduction.

Assurance

Supplier oversight

Challenge MSP access, security controls, escalation routes, contractual obligations and evidence standards.

Operations

Recovery confidence

Ensure recovery order, restore testing, communications, rota planning and business continuity are rehearsed.

Culture

Human risk reduction

Support awareness, reporting culture, phishing resistance and staff wellbeing during high-pressure incidents.

Board reporting

What should be reported monthly?

A board dashboard should avoid technical noise and show whether the organisation is becoming safer, faster to recover and better governed.

MFA coverage

Percentage of cloud, admin, VPN and supplier access protected by MFA.

Restore confidence

Last successful restore test, scope covered and unresolved backup risks.

Privileged access

Number of active admin accounts, supplier exceptions and overdue reviews.

Training and reporting

Awareness completion, phishing reporting rate and recurring behavioural gaps.

Supplier assurance

Critical suppliers reviewed, evidence received and unacceptable exceptions.

Incident readiness

Tabletop completion, decision-log quality and open corrective actions.

The CIO message to the owner.

Cyber resilience is not optional technology spend. It is a business continuity, customer trust, legal exposure and revenue protection programme that must be governed at board level.
