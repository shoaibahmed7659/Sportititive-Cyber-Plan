Attack Analysis

The Sportetitive attack: how a single email became a ransomware crisis

This section maps the full attack chain against MITRE ATT&CK and the Cyber Kill Chain framework. Understanding every step — from the first LinkedIn post to the ransom demand — is essential to building defences that would have stopped it and to ensuring the board understands its true exposure.

Cyber Kill Chain phase mapping

Every action the attacker took maps to one of the seven Kill Chain phases. Disrupting any phase would have limited or prevented the attack.

1

Reconnaissance

Pete Mitchell's LinkedIn post named the charity, his role, his email address, and mentioned the CEO by name. This gave attackers everything they needed to build a targeted spear-phishing campaign at zero cost. Passive OSINT, no network contact required.

T1589 Gather Victim IdentityT1593 Search Social Media
2

Weaponisation

A fake website impersonating the Sportetitive intranet or supplier portal was created. A custom spear-phishing email was crafted using the CEO's name and a plausible business reason to visit the site. This required minimal technical skill but high knowledge of the target.

T1566.002 Spear Phishing LinkT1608 Stage Capabilities
3

Delivery

The phishing email was sent directly to Pete Mitchell on 19 February 2026. He clicked the link, entered his MSP credentials on the fake site, and an initial foothold was established. No antivirus alert was triggered because the link itself was to an external server — not a malicious file.

T1566.001 PhishingT1078 Valid Accounts
4

Exploitation & Installation

Using Pete's MSP credentials, attackers logged into Sportetitive's infrastructure via the MSP's remote management tools. No vulnerability exploit was needed — legitimate access routes were used. A keylogger was installed on the IT Manager's machine through the MSP toolset.

T1133 External Remote ServicesT1056.001 KeyloggingT1543 Create System Service
5

Command & Control

With the IT Manager's domain administrator credentials captured via keylogger on 23 February, attackers established persistent C2 access. They moved laterally across the network, exploring systems over a two-week period without triggering alerts, because no MFA was required and behaviour-based monitoring was absent.

T1071 Application Layer Protocol C2T1021.001 RDP Lateral MovementT1040 Network Sniffing
6

Actions on Objectives — Exfiltration

Before deploying ransomware, attackers exfiltrated 60,000+ customer records and all employee records including payroll data. This is the “double extortion” model: encrypt and steal. Data theft confirms the attacker had read access to customer and HR databases — both processed under UK GDPR.

T1486 Data Encrypted for ImpactT1048 Exfiltration Over Alternative ProtocolT1530 Data from Cloud Storage
7

Actions on Objectives — Encryption & Ransom

On 8 March 2026 at 20:23, ransomware was deployed using AES-256, RSA, and Curve25519 encryption (Conti variant). IMS, website databases, core file servers, and backup access were encrypted. A ransom demand for 1.3 BTC was sent via the compromised CEO email account the following morning.

ICO obligations triggered

The exfiltration of personal data creates legal obligations that Sportetitive must meet regardless of the operational recovery timeline.

72-hour notification (Article 33 / DPA 2018 s.108)

Where a personal data breach is likely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals, the ICO must be notified without undue delay and, where feasible, within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach.

ICO breach notification portal →

Individual notification (Article 34)

If the breach is likely to result in high risk to individuals (fraud, identity theft), those individuals must be notified directly and without undue delay. 60,000+ customer records breached meets this threshold.

ICO guidance on individual notification →

Potential enforcement action

The ICO can issue enforcement notices, monetary penalties (up to £17.5m or 4% of global turnover for the most serious breaches), and public reprimands. A cooperative, transparent response demonstrably reduces enforcement risk.

ICO guide to data breach obligations →

DPA 2018 s.108 — UK-specific obligation

The Data Protection Act 2018, section 108 mirrors the UK GDPR Article 33 obligation but applies to all UK data controllers, including those processing data solely within the UK. This applies to Sportetitive's employee records as well as customer data.

Business impact by function

The ransomware attack disrupted operations across every part of Sportetitive's business simultaneously, creating cascading failures that a single-point response would not address.

Trading operations

  • All six stores closed for at least 24 hours
  • E-commerce platform offline for 11 days
  • Manual stock management until IMS restoration
  • Estimated £480,000 in trading revenue lost

Customer trust

  • 60,000+ customers notified of data exposure
  • Social media posts from customers within hours
  • Negative press coverage during ICO investigation
  • Customer retention risk during recovery period

Regulatory

  • ICO formal investigation opened
  • Mandatory 72-hour notification deadline activated
  • Potential enforcement action if response inadequate
  • Obligation to notify individuals at high risk

Financial

  • £1.3m total incident cost to date
  • Forensic specialists, legal, IT overtime
  • Insurance claim processing delays
  • Increased cyber insurance premiums expected

People

  • All 125 employees' personal data breached
  • IT team under extreme pressure for 72+ hours
  • Store staff and management distress and uncertainty
  • CHRO welfare support obligation activated

Supplier and MSP trust

  • Spherical Services access suspended mid-incident
  • MSP security practices now under formal review
  • Supplier governance gaps exposed
  • POS vendor relationship and SphereSales v4 risk identified
