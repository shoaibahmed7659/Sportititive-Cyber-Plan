Attack narrative

How the incident escalated from phishing and trust abuse into a board-level crisis

The attackers did not need a highly advanced entry technique. They combined social engineering, supplier access, weak identity control, privileged account exposure, and delayed detection to turn one compromise into widespread disruption and a major personal data breach.

01

Initial targeting

The attackers used publicly available information and supplier relationships to craft a believable approach to an MSP technician, showing how external trust can become internal risk without strong verification.

02

Credential compromise

Malware deployed through the phishing route later captured highly privileged credentials, creating a major escalation point across systems, cloud services and core infrastructure.

03

Lateral movement

Once inside, the attackers explored the environment, disabled protections, and moved across systems without being stopped early because access was too broad and visibility was too weak.

04

Operational disruption

Files were encrypted, the website and IMS became unavailable, online trading was interrupted, and executives faced time-sensitive decisions even while some store functions remained partially operational.

05

Data breach impact

Investigators later confirmed that personal data relating to at least 60,000 customers and all employees had been exposed, transforming the incident into a serious legal and reputational event.

06

Business consequences

The attack produced recovery costs, staff fatigue, insurer involvement, reputational damage, disrupted operations, and ongoing anxiety about regulatory scrutiny and future trust.

The real lesson is that weak trust boundaries and privileged access design can magnify a small entry point into enterprise-wide harm.

That is why the response should not focus only on malware removal. It must also address supplier assurance, identity governance, detection capability, and the quality of executive decision-making under pressure.
