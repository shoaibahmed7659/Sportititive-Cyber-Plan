Framework view

MITRE ATT&CK explains the attack path, while Zero Trust explains the control response

MITRE ATT&CK helps the board understand how the attackers moved from initial contact to business impact. Zero Trust then provides a practical control narrative built on strong authentication, least privilege, segmentation, monitoring, and supplier verification.

Reconnaissance

Initial access through trust

The attackers used public information and a convincing phishing route through a supplier relationship. The response lesson is that external trust should never replace proper verification.

Credential access

Identity became the breach multiplier

Captured privileged credentials allowed the attackers to avoid repeated intrusion effort, showing why MFA, admin separation, and access review should be central control priorities.

Lateral movement

Broad access enabled spread

Insufficient segmentation allowed the attackers to move further than they should have. Zero Trust reduces this by limiting what users, devices, and suppliers can reach.

Exfiltration

Data theft raised legal exposure

The theft of personal data changed the event from outage management to regulatory risk, making monitoring, classification, and transfer visibility more important.

Impact

Encryption created board pressure

The encryption of systems disrupted operations and intensified executive time pressure, reinforcing the need for tested backups, recovery order, and business continuity alignment.

Zero Trust

Control strategy for the future

Zero Trust gives the CIO a business-friendly narrative: every user, device, supplier session, and administrative action should be verified, limited, and monitored.
